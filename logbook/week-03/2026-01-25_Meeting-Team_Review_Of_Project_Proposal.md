## 📝 Discussion Summary
The primary purpose of this meeting was to go through the first draft of the Project Proposal Report as a team and identify potential gaps/inconsistencies/areas of improvement 
with respect to meeting the rubric expectations. Additionally, a specific focus was placed on proper flow between sections, formatting/referencing, and clarity in
the design summary.

---

### Details of the Discussion

**Context:**  
Initially, each team member read through all sections of the report they did not contribute to, and while doing so, they evaluated each section against the rubric's
requirements and took notes on areas to improve. Then, the team went through the report, one section at a time, and each individual shared their notes/evaluations
for that section. All feedback was considered, debated, and assigned to the person who wrote that section. While there were many points discussed and noted for these
sections, which would be overwhelming to include in a logbook summary, the section below summarizes some of the key issues identified.

**Key Issues Discussed/Identified:**
- The team realized that the first section of the report, _Proponent Background and Understanding of The Problem_, was missing direct citation references in the discussion
of the research topics the team explored. Since this is an essential aspect of the rubric for this section, and for appropriate formatting, this was corrected.
Additionally, the team found that this section was overlapping with the system architecture and implementation sections, so for concision, the hardware/technical heavy
details were cut down. 
- For the planning and control subsystems, they had not defined how they were going to quantitatively measure performance, and what benchmarks/requirements they
would evaluate these metrics against. Upon recognizing this gap, the team engaged in meaningful discussions of performance metrics and benchmarks for these two
subsystems, and this was added to the report.
- In a few sections, the team discovered the use of the first person tense by accident, which was corrected.
- In regard to formatting, some members of the team were not using the built-in Word references for figures and tables, so the team had a discussion and standardized
the use of these tools for appropriate referencing in reports.
- When going through the risk section, the team engaged in meaningful discussions, which helped identify new risks and mitigation strategies that weren't considered 
before. Consequently, this improved the depth of this section.

**Decisions Made & Next Steps:**
- Upon going through the feedback for all sections, each team member is to improve their sections accordingly.
- Once the feedback is incorporated, the team will review the section again individually and raise any concerns about issues that may still be lingering.

### Overall Reflection
Engaging in this team review session was a profoundly beneficial activity because it allowed the team to effectively identify gaps in the report with respect to the
rubric expectations. Discussing and debating each other's feedback notes enabled meaningful conversations, which generated new or more effective ideas, and helped 
improve the quality of the report submitted. Furthermore, by standardizing good report writing practices in this review session, this will help the team with future 
written deliverables by ensuring that each member is on the same page with respect to how formatting and referencing should be completed. Consequently, this will 
save valuable time when editing future written deliverables. Overall, it is important to edit/go through written deliverables as a team because it helps ensure cohesion
in writing and helps identify areas of improvement that an individual may have neglected or missed when working by themselves.
TLDR: Group editing is immensely valuable in identifying shortcomings.
